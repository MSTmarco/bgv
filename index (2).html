The Problem: Methane Leaks Are Expensive and Invisible

  • 95% of natural gas is methane (CH₄).
  • Methane has ~80× the warming impact of CO₂ and persists for ~12 years — our most powerful short-term climate lever.
  • Oil & Gas accounts for ~15% of global anthropogenic methane emissions.
  • Operators can lose up to 6% of their gas through leaks — often ~USD 30M per year per company.
  • Current detection methods are expensive, fragmented, and unreliable at scale.

Today's Tools: Sensors, Aircraft, and Gaps

CURRENT METHODS

  • Satellites — expensive, detect only super-emitters, weather-dependent.
  • Drones — non-continuous, weather-dependent, special certifications.
  • Light aircraft — expensive, not continuous, heavily regulated.
  • Handheld sensors — manual, slow, weather-dependent.
  • Fixed sensors — expensive to scale and maintain.

THE PROBLEM

These tools are reactive:

  • They only confirm a leak while measuring.
  • Most cannot determine duration.
  • None reliably capture intermittent leaks.
Methane leaks typically last 3–5 hours (Wang et al., 2022; Stokes et al., 2022). To catch every leak, you'd need continuous sensors everywhere — costing millions.

Our Solution: Predictive Agents Using Operational Data

Leaks follow patterns in operational data before they occur. We detect those patterns using AI and Machine Learning.
